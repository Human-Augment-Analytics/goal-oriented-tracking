# Management & Leadership Mid-Semester Report — Operations Questions

## Define what reoccurring tasks you are doing within your project group.

The following recurring tasks are performed on a regular basis to keep the project group organized and on track:

- **Weekly group meeting** — Attending the standing meeting with the full research group to review progress, surface blockers, and align on upcoming work.
- **Meeting notes** — Taking and distributing notes during weekly group meeting to ensure decisions and action items are documented and accessible.
- **Slack monitoring** — Actively monitoring the group Slack channel to stay current on individual researcher updates, questions, and async progress between meetings.
- **Weekly progress tracking** — Logging and maintaining a weekly work tracking spreadsheet to record researcher activity and goal progress. [View Tracking Sheet](https://gtvault-my.sharepoint.com/:x:/g/personal/mcosta35_gatech_edu/IQCcDIQLPuJVSKWBPgkVgGCkAapcDfmTEzvk963rdX38Ygs?e=auhyic)
- **Weekly initiative meeting with lead researcher** — A recently introduced one-on-one sync with the lead researcher to review initiative-level progress and ensure alignment on goals. *(Just started)*



## How are you assessing your role as the project manager and its effect on the overall project?

The CDDM group is a mature research team that is actively progressing toward publication. Given this, my role as project manager is less about driving direction and more about ensuring operations run smoothly and nothing falls through the cracks.

My primary metric for success is whether I am helping things move without friction. In practice, this means monitoring meetings, facilitating weekly communications, and making sure no individual researcher gets stuck or blocked without a timely response.

**Success criteria:** As long as there are no outstanding communication or administrative issues that persist for more than one week, I assess my contributions as positive and effective. The absence of prolonged blockers or unresolved coordination gaps is the clearest signal that my role is having a meaningful impact on the group's progress.



## What challenges and successes have you observed in your group?

**Challenges:**

When a research direction did not yield the expected results, the team spent several weeks working through how to pivot and continue progressing toward publication. This period of uncertainty slowed momentum and highlighted a gap in how the group responds to negative results. This is a challenge that could potentially have been resolved more quickly with a more structured initiative tracking process — specifically the goal-first tracking initiative proposed in the accompanying document.

**Successes:**

- Strong communication between researchers throughout the semester, with minimal coordination breakdowns.
- Demonstrated leadership among researchers in self-organizing and driving their own work forward.
- Successfully completed a model reassessment and pivoted research direction after the initial approach did not pan out.
- When the deadline for the first target journal was not met, the group was able to adapt and redirect efforts toward an alternative publication — showing resilience and flexibility as a team.



## How are you collaborating with stakeholders to ensure your role is supporting and motivating the group?

Stakeholder collaboration happens through several channels:

- **One-on-one communication** — Regular async and synchronous check-ins with the lead researcher and/or professor throughout the week to stay aligned on priorities and surface any issues early.
- **Weekly group meetings** — Consistent attendance at the full team meeting to maintain visibility into group dynamics and researcher progress.
- **Weekly one-on-one sync with the lead researcher** — A dedicated recurring sync to review initiative-level progress and ensure the lead researcher has the support needed to keep the team moving forward.
- **Initiative kickoff meeting** — Led a dedicated meeting with the entire team to introduce the goal-oriented tracking initiative and walk through the Slack-based Kanban tools that were set up to support it. This helped ensure the team understood the new process and had an opportunity to ask questions and engage with the system directly.



## What feedback have you received on your role, solicited or unsolicited?

Given the maturity of the CDDM group, feedback has been largely positive and centered around continuity. The team has expressed appreciation that I have not introduced disruptive changes to their established workflow, allowing them to stay focused on their research.

One notable piece of solicited feedback came during the initiative kickoff meeting. The lead researcher raised a concern about the existing weekly progress report format, noting that the PDF submission process and its required outline were too time-consuming relative to the value they provide. This feedback has been noted and will inform how the reporting process is streamlined going forward — ideally replacing it with a lighter-weight format that still captures meaningful progress without creating unnecessary overhead for the researchers. However, another Management student is undertaking this initiative.



## What plans do you have for enhancing your role as the project manager with the remaining time in the semester?

For the remainder of the semester, the focus will be on two key areas:

- **Weekly initiative meetings with the lead researcher** — Continuing and building on the recently established one-on-one syncs to maintain alignment on initiative progress and ensure any blockers are addressed quickly.
- **Tracking velocity toward goals** — Actively monitoring how fast the team is moving toward its defined goals and using that data to identify risks to deadlines early, so adjustments can be made before issues compound.



## Please provide feedback on what support you may need in your role.

The primary feedback for the Management team is to allow for more interactive, student-driven time during the weekly Management meetings. Creating space for students to lead discussions, raise topics, and engage more actively would make these sessions more valuable and better reflect the collaborative nature of the research environment.
